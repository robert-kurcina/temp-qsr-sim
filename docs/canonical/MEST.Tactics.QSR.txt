 
MEST
Matter. Energy. Space. Time.

QUICK START RULES
Welcome to the MEST Tactics (‚ÄúMEST‚Äù) miniatures rules system. MEST is an acronym meaning ‚Äúmatter, energy, space, and time‚Äù. 
It is a ‚Äúmodels-agnostic‚Äù toolkit miniatures gaming system.
Overview
These are the Quick Start Rules. They are just enough of the fundamentals to prepare players to learn the more complete set of rules for the MEST Tactics Core book. Rules for Firearms, Grenades, and Spells have been removed.
This version of the QSR limits the rules to common battles waged by combatants of the Bronze Age (~2250 CE) up to the Renaissance Age (~1300 CE).
Which Miniatures to Use?
The players should gather 10 to 20 models. These should be of the common fantasy or medieval sculpts, and as such be armed with swords, daggers, spears, and even bows or crossbows. 
Which Terrain to Use?
The players can just gather some books, boxes, and other shapes to layout and stack together to form their ‚Äúbattlefields‚Äù.
What is the Goal?
For these QSR rules, the goal is; create an ‚Äúassembly‚Äù of combatants for each player, learning the rules, and dealing out mayhem and death while resolving the default game ‚Äúmission‚Äù known as the ‚ÄúElimination‚Äù.
How to Learn?
Scan through the rules. Try them out. Make mistakes. Do it again. Afterwards, try out the Intermediate and Advanced rules which are marked with triangles (ÔÅ∞) and (ÔÅ≤).

 
Materials Required for Play
Players need to provide various materials to begin a game.
Dice
Acquire at least 6 dice in each of the three (red, white, yellow) dice colors (18 dice total).
ÔÇß	Commonly available regular six-sided dice with pips (dots) numbering 1 to 6 will be adequate.
ÔÇß	Use red for Modifier dice, white as Base dice, and yellow for Wild dice. These are also known as m, b, and w dice.
Have a few different colors of tiny utility six-side dice. These are used for various purposes such as for End-game dice, count-down timers, etc.
Optionally use a very large six-sided dice to track the current game-turn ‚ÄúTurn‚Äù. These dice are big so that all players can see them at a distance if necessary.
Models
At least 4 models per player in a variety of sculpts. 
We recommend 28MM or 1:56 scale figurines, but the game can support nearly any scale. These 1:56 scale models should be mounted upon 30MM (or use 1.25-inch) circular bases if human-sized (‚ÄúHumanoid‚Äù SIZ 3).
If the models are already based upon something different such as ovals, squares or hexagons, this is acceptable; just presume the rules are written for those bases.
Battlefield
Every battlefield will be dynamically configured by all players involved, using available terrain elements.
ÔÇß	A play-area, or ‚Äúbattlefield‚Äù of at least 24" √ó 24" in dimensions. The ideal size is 48" √ó 48". Allow additional room for side-boarded cards, utilities, and play-aids. 
ÔÇß	Lots of terrain representing trees, buildings, hills, walls, and clutter. Allow 1 to 4 Items per 12"√ó12" play-area.
Tools
ÔÇß	A pair of measuring sticks 8 MU long divided into 1 MU segments. Each measured unit (1 MU) is about 1" long, which is the width of a standard circular base. At one end should be a marking at 0.5 MU.
ÔÇß	Markers and tokens of various colors; beads are extremely useful for this purpose.
ÔÇß	A couple of gaming pawns to serve as reminders.
ÔÇß	Paper and pencils or pens for record keeping. 
ÔÇß	Although not necessary, it is very useful to have at least one laser-pointer to check ‚ÄúLine-of-Sight‚Äù [ LOS ] during game-play.
ÔÇß	Another nice-to-have are tiny Japanese-style side-dish bowls for rolling dice in hand, or some dice-trays.
Tokens
During game-play a character will succumb to one or more common status effects. Such information could be recorded on paper, but it is much more useful to identify models by placing colored glass beads as tokens, or status markers next to them. 
If using character cards, place the tokens upon those cards to keep the play-area clutter-free.
Here are the colors and sizes that are recommended as the standard for play. 
ÔÇß	Done 		‚Äî Largest & blue
ÔÇß	Wait 		‚Äî Big & white or clear
ÔÇß	Hidden 	‚Äî Small & dark
ÔÇß	Wound 	‚Äî Small & red
ÔÇß	Delay 	‚Äî Small & white or clear
ÔÇß	Fear 		‚Äî Small & yellow
Markers
There should be some markers to indicate that a weapon (a bow or crossbow) is ‚ÄúOut-of-Ammo!‚Äù to represent that they must reload before using again. These are best represented using icons upon small chits, washers, or bingo chips which are about 20MM or 0.75‚Äù across.
There are also tactical resource types such as ‚ÄúInitiative Points‚Äù. These will be held near a player as spendable resources. These are best represented using icons upon larger elements which are about 30MM or 1.25‚Äù across, or larger.
Proxies
Not every tabletop wargame enthusiast has all of the necessary game-pieces, models, and terrain elements to build a complete set for any tabletop wargame. For the most part, TTWGs can be played using proxies. Use cups and books for terrain, coins for models, or paper cut-outs for nearly everything. For models, good sources are the many available ‚Äúdudes on a map‚Äù boardgames. 
Having artful pieces representing each type of game element helps tremendously with immersion by putting less strain on the imagination, allowing players to focus on tactics and the rules which support that.
 
BASIC GAME
Learn these first and try out the Intermediate (ÔÅ≤) and the Advanced Game (ÔÅ∞) rules later. 
Assemblies
Players are given Build Points [ BP ] to purchase several characters and their weapons, armor, and equipment. Together these characters form what are known as Assemblies. Each player receives their own Assembly.
Building an Assembly
Before players build their Assemblies, they should agree on how big of a game to play, which genre, and which Mission. Afterwards they can choose which side [ Side ] their Assembly will represent. 
Genres & Settings
Genres are a collection of thematic rules and guidelines for containing specific settings. A setting specifies the technological period which limits available weapons, armor, and equipment. It may also limit what kinds of combatants (‚Äúarchetypes‚Äù), be they orcs, marines, or androids, can appear.
Essentially players using just this rule book can do the following whenever playing in a new gaming session:
ÔÇß	Pick a genre and a setting within it. 
ÔÇß	Agree as to which weapons and armors are available.
ÔÇß	Agree to which archetypes (more below) are available.
Choosing Sides
ÔÇß	A game is played using at least two opposing sides with a single player for each Side. That‚Äôs usually two players.
ÔÇß	Characters on the same Side are Friendly and if not then they are Opposing and are considered opponents.
Game Size
MEST Tactics games are usually rated as Small, Medium, or Large. This helps players estimate the time to play and is decided by the total number of models used for a game session. 
In a game with two sides (‚ÄúSides‚Äù), the total number of models divided by two gives the rough Game Size. Game size determines the general amount of BP per Side. Experienced players may instead want to decide Game Size ahead of time, and limit models and BP as a result.
For these QSR rules, presume that the Game Size will always be Small. This limits the number of models to 4 to 8 per Side, and the number of BP per Side is about 250 to 500. It should take between 30 to 60 minutes to play.
 
Ad Hoc Game Size
Players might not know how large of a game (‚ÄúGame Size‚Äù) they‚Äôll want to play, and so instead could agree to a minimum model count. 
ÔÇß	Each Side forms their Assembly using the minimum model count. Afterwards determine the total BP for those Sides. 
ÔÇß	Total the number of models and the BP for all Sides and divide each total by two. Find the Game Size by using the nearest ‚ÄúModels per Side‚Äù and then the ‚ÄúBP per Side‚Äù columns.
Equivalent Forces
Depending on the genre, the number of characters which could be purchased and equipped with 250 BP could be a dozen or maybe just one.
ÔÇß	Be sure all Sides meet the minimum and maximum model count and the maximum BP allotment.
ÔÇß	If either the allowed BP or the model count needs to be reconsidered, be sure to ask the other player(s) first.
ÔÇß	If more BP is needed to have the minimum models, allot each Side the necessary BP amount, and round up to the nearest 50 BP. Repeat if necessary.
ÔÇß	Try to keep the total BP spent by each Side ideally within 25 BP of each other and the BP allotment. A difference could be as high as 5 BP per 100 BP allotted to any Side and things will remain roughly competitive.
Multiple Players
ÔÇß	Three or more players can participate in a game session. 
ÔÇß	In this case players should agree to the available sides and each player may then pick which Side their Assemblies will call their own. 
Multiple Sides
MEST Tactics can support three or more opposing sides (‚ÄúSide‚Äù) but try to have even numbers of opposing sides in order to avoid ‚Äúplayer bashing‚Äù.
ÔÇß	A Side may support any number of players but try to keep them equivalent in order to maintain balance.
ÔÇß	As a method to help keep games manageable in size when there are three or more sides; double the total number of BP and models available and divide them between all available sides.

Attacker vs. Defender
The Attacker is generally the character who is performing a Test against another character or model which is the Defender.
Attribute Values
The average attribute value for a human character is 2. Each point higher roughly doubles that attribute's effectiveness. 
ÔÇß	The normal human attribute value range is from 1 to 3.
ÔÉº	Exceptional humans, like those most often found in pulp action novels or science-fantasy movies, are at the peak of capability and are rated at a 4. 
ÔÉº	Any attribute with a value of 5 and higher for ‚Äúreal life‚Äù humans is in the realm of superheroes, sci-fi, or high fantasy. 
ÔÇß	When comparing the attribute values between any two characters, a difference of 1 is significant, 2 is outstanding, and 3 or more is often insurmountable.
Reducing
Whenever an Attribute value is ‚Äúreduced‚Äù, it is lowered towards zero and no further. If an Attribute value is already below zero, reducing it will not affect it. So, reducing a value of 3 alters it to become a 2, but reducing a value of -1 has no effect and it remains as a -1.
Dashed Attribute Values
Some archetypes have attribute values showing dashes as in ‚Äò-‚Äò, ‚Äò=‚Äô, or ‚Äò‚â°‚Äô. These represent -1, -2, and -3 respectively.
The SIZ Attribute
The SIZ (‚Äúsize‚Äù) attribute is used to represent how much physical punishment (‚ÄúDamage‚Äù) a character can take before becoming KO‚Äôd (‚ÄúKnocked-out‚Äù) or Eliminated. 
ÔÇß	Most adult humanoid characters are SIZ 3.
ÔÉº	SIZ increases by 1 for every doubling of a character‚Äôs mass in kilograms (‚ÄúKg of mass‚Äù). 
ÔÉº	SIZ 1 is about 20 Kg of mass, SIZ 2 is about 50 Kg of mass, and SIZ of 3 is about 100 Kg. A SIZ 4 character is about 200 Kg, and SIZ 5 characters are about 500 Kg.
ÔÇß	The largest practical model to use in battles is SIZ 6 which is about 1000 Kg; this is mostly to represent giants, horses, or very large monsters. Smallest is SIZ 1.
Base-contact & Base-height
When the bases of two models touch, they are in ‚Äúbase-contact‚Äù. Bases of models should not overlap those of other models especially between models of characters which are hostile to each other (‚ÄúOpposing‚Äù).
Having base-diameters vary with SIZ allows for models which are on smaller bases to greatly outnumber models of larger bases when in base-contact.
A model‚Äôs base-height is its base-diameter if Humanoid, and Beasts are about half the normal base-height. 
Cylindrical Volume
The volume of a model is standardized as the imaginary cylinder created from its base-diameter and its base-height. This is necessary because some models are sculpted in a dynamic pose hopping over terrain, and others are sculpted leaning forward or perhaps slinking very low to the ground.
Melee Range ‚Äî A target is within Melee Range if its volume is in base-contact with an Opposing model's volume.

Archetypes
Archetypes are a sort of template used to quickly define what a character can or cannot do. It is a name followed by nine attributes, a list of traits, and a BP cost value. 
Written Presentation
Characters are presented in written form in a multiple of ways. The standard concise format displays the name of the archetype followed by its 9 attributes written as a ‚Äústat-line‚Äù of triplets between vertical lines like so:
CCA RCA REF | INT POW STR | FOR MOV SIZ
After the archetype and attributes line the format identifies the species and its BP cost, followed by all of its traits. 
Here‚Äôs an example of the Average Common archetype with a stat-line showing 2 for all Attributes except for SIZ which is shown as a value of 3. It is Humanoid (representing a generic human-like species) and has no traits. It costs 30 BP.

Assigning Archetypes
At the start of each MEST Tactics game session a given model can be assigned a different archetype than in a previous session. What could be assigned will change according to the needs of the controlling player. 
ÔÅ∂	The basic premise is that the players shouldn‚Äôt become necessarily attached to what a model looks like and should concern themselves with what the archetypes assigned to a model could instead do. 
ÔÅ∂	It is reasonable that the model in a previous session was assigned the Brawler archetype and now it is instead qualified as a Hero or Leader archetype.
Any two nearly identical models with the same sex, uniform type and color, equipment, species, etc. should be given the same assigned Archetype within any given Mission. If they are different, be sure to identify them clearly and inform the other players.
 
The Common Archetypes
Common archetypes represent the majority of humanoid characters encountered in most genres. Unless identified elsewhere or made more obvious in its archetype naming, most archetypes are presumed to ‚ÄúHumanoid‚Äù with its species (‚ÄúSpecies‚Äù) as Human.
The Common archetypes are listed in increasing order of cost in BP below;
ÔÇß	Untrained ‚Äî This is for most civilians and other non-combatants. Ideal for bystanders and librarians.
ÔÇß	Militia ‚Äî Recruits, poorly trained soldiers, and conscripts. This can ideally represent teenagers drafted from war-torn cities, towns, and farmlands.
ÔÇß	Average ‚Äî This archetype represents the most encountered combatant or adventurer. 
ÔÇß	Veteran ‚Äî The grizzled professional soldier. They‚Äôve seen combat enough to not be too stirred by it.
ÔÇß	Elite ‚Äî Representing the most highly trained combatants in any Assembly. Cinematic assassins.

Variant Archetypes
Each of the Common archetypes have a few variants known either as Variant Common archetypes. 
These variants are the standard Common archetypes plus one or more additional traits indicated by a small plus (+) sign before the addition. If an Archetype already has such a trait, the addition increases the trait level by one. 
The Variant archetype name is primary archetype (such as Average or Untrained) plus its variant archetype.
Here are some examples:
ÔÇß	Average, Brawny ‚Äî An Average archetype but with the addition of the Brawn trait to represent a big, strong, character. Brawn only aids in ways not involving causing Damage during combat.
ÔÇß	Average, Archer ‚Äî An Average archetype but with the addition of the Archery trait to represent the very rapid archery ability of ancient archers. Does not receive the Shoot trait.
ÔÇß	Average, Cultist ‚Äî Nearly all Cultists have the Insane trait which protects them from the Fear trait.
ÔÇß	Marksman, Archer ‚Äî The Marksman archetype which has the Shoot trait, and also the Archery trait.
ÔÇß	Untrained, Scholarly ‚Äî Represents the invaluable but nerdy professor; book-smart but not able to participate in combat too much. Receives the Scholar trait.
 
Traits
Traits are assigned to characters, weapons, armor, and equipment. Each trait is a set of one or more small rules that enhances the main set of rules or alter the effects of another trait. Near the back of this rules book is a Traits List section showing for how each modifies the game rules.
Overview 
ÔÇß	Something with a given trait can be referenced with that trait. So, a weapon with the [Flint] trait is a Flint weapon. If a trait states that there is a "target", this may refer to other models or to locations. Most traits are abilities that provide beneficial effects.
ÔÇß	Some traits are disabilities which have penalty effects. Disabilities are identified within square brackets such as [Blinders], [Stub], or [Coward]. Related disabilities are written within the same bracket [Stub, Awkward].
ÔÇß	Some trait entries are given an X such as Armor X or [Laden X]. These allow traits to be acquired at a certain level; the default level for such traits is always 1. 
ÔÇß	Some traits have variations such as [Discard+] or [Discard!]. These are [Discard] but with extra clauses. 
ÔÇß	Traits may have classifier keywords such as ‚ÄúMovement‚Äù, ‚ÄúAoE‚Äù, ‚ÄúSkill‚Äù, ‚ÄúIntrinsic‚Äù, ‚ÄúAsset‚Äù, ‚ÄúAttack Effect‚Äù, ‚ÄúPsychology‚Äù, ‚ÄúNatural Weapon‚Äù, or ‚ÄúMagic‚Äù which are hooks for other game rules.
ÔÇß	Traits may have a ‚Äúsee‚Äù trait reference to another trait such as ‚ÄúThrowable ‚Äî Asset. see [Discard]‚Äù. The referenced trait may also apply whenever using the main trait depending on the context of that trait. 
ÔÇß	If a reference has an X in it such as ‚ÄúStealthy X ‚Äî see Night-vision X‚Äù it means that the referenced trait will be at the same level as the main trait.
ÔÇß	Some traits have an arrow (‚Äú>‚Äù) which indicates that it is affected by whatever item or traits exist at the right of the arrow, such as Claws > Poison, or Fume > Smoke. 
ÔÉº	Often what is on the right of the arrow is a dependency. Claws > Poison indicates that the Poison trait is active only when the Claws are used.
ÔÉº	Sometimes what is on the right of the arrow is a list delimited by periods and written within curly-braces such as when using weapons with the ROF trait:
ROF > {[Jitter, Feed, Jam]. Burst}

Adding Trait Levels
Whenever a trait with levels is acquired more than once to a given thing, be it a character, weapon, armor or equipment; the trait levels are added together for its default usage of that thing.
For example; a character with the Medium Armor has 
AR 4 [ ‚ÄúArmor Rating 4‚Äù ] which when combined with a Medium Shield that has AR 1 and so the total is AR 5.
Reducing Trait Levels
Whenever a trait mentions ‚Äúreduce‚Äù, it will decrease the levels of something such as dice or a named trait by a certain amount for a very specific context. If this reduces that thing to zero or less, then regard that as not having any in-game effect for the duration of that context.
Applying Traits
Traits normally affect just that character, weapon, armor, or equipment to which they‚Äôve been assigned.
ÔÇß	Ability traits apply whenever desired by the player controlling the model, weapon, armor, or equipment.
ÔÇß	Disability traits are applied according to their descriptions. All players should take note to always apply Disability traits as often as necessary.
ÔÇß	Traits always over-ride non-Trait rules but most often augment each other. Conflicting traits should be decided in favor of the Active character. Or roll a die to decide.

 
Purchasing Characters
Players spend their allotted Build Points [ BP ] to assign their characters an archetype. 
Archetype Rarity
The Common archetypes should be adequate for most introductory games. These are Humans or humanoids.
WYSIWYG
MEST Tactics encourages frequent use of the WYSIWYG (wiz-ee-wig) rule or ‚ÄúWhat you see is what you get.‚Äù 
ÔÇß	Try to use models that are sculpted to represent what they are to be. 
ÔÉº	Want a werewolf? use a werewolf model. 
ÔÉº	Want a thug with a sword? use a model with a sword. 
ÔÉº	Want an assassin with a rifle? use a model with a rifle.
ÔÇß	Acquiring physical models with very specific combinations of features sculpted into them for any player is hard to do. Players should be relatively flexible in interpreting what passes as WYSIWYG. 
ÔÉº	A ‚Äúsword‚Äù could be a Broadsword, a Longsword, a Machete, etc. A ‚Äúpistol‚Äù in hand could be a Revolver, an Auto-pistol, or a Semi-automatic.
ÔÉº	Some Items, such as Daggers or Torches, are assigned the Conceal or the Discrete traits which disregard the WYSIWYG limitations. Equipment is given Conceal by default.
Character Profiles
A profile (‚ÄúProfile‚Äù) is an Archetype combined with all assigned Items of weapons, armor, and equipment. Profiles are identified with a name such as ‚ÄúRed shirt with sword‚Äù, or even ‚ÄúMary Sue‚Äù. At this point, they‚Äôll also become known as characters (‚ÄúCharacters‚Äù).
Character Record Sheet
Attached to the rules are ‚ÄúCharacter Record Sheets‚Äù which will aide in tracking information for each player‚Äôs Assembly. See the section on Additional Materials.
Templates and Frames
An Archetype representing the most basic elements of a species is a "template", such as the Common Average archetype for Humans. Frames are additions to an existing Archetype to make them Variants.
 
Outfitting Characters
Players may spend BP to purchase and assign Items (‚ÄúItems‚Äù) to their characters, which are weapons, armors, and equipment.
ÔÇß	Assign Items reasonably to characters according to how they are sculpted. 
ÔÇß	All characters, weapons, armor, and equipment are rated with a BP cost. 
For example; a Common Average character costs 30 BP and a Medium Revolver is 33 BP. Together this character when armed with the weapon is 63 BP. 
For example; a Common Untrained character costs 7 BP and Daggers cost 6 BP. Together this costs 13 BP.
ÔÇß	Characters and Items are also given traits which are little rules that alter or break the rules in the game. Most traits, the Ability traits, are beneficial, but Disability traits have penalty effects. See the Traits List section for more information.
Available Hands
A character is allowed a number of hands (‚ÄúHand‚Äù) equal to its model's sculpt, including prehensile tails and trunks, tentacles, or other. All Humanoid characters are presumed to have two Hands and can normally wield one Item each.
ÔÇß	Items require a commitment of 1 or 2 hands, noted as [1H] and [2H]. These then become ‚ÄúIn Hand‚Äù.
ÔÇß	All Fiddle actions must commit 1 available Hand each.
ÔÇß	A character may commit with one less available Hand, but this causes a penalty of -1 Base die for any Fiddle Tests, and for the very next Test performed when interrupted by a React.  
See the list of Traits regarding the [1H] and [2H] traits for more information on how this affects game-play.
A Common Example:
The most common example is outfitting a character with a Shield [1H] and a Spear [2H]. 
This would require three Hands, but most humanoid characters have just two. This character can use the Spear with one fewer Hand. However, if it is interrupted and attacked during a React it will suffer a -1 Base die penalty.
Another Common Example:
A character could be outfitted with multiple items requiring Hands. Such as having Longbow and a Dagger, or having two Spears. A player may decide that the additional items are stowed somewhere on their person. They may use the Fiddle action for each item or pair to switch out.
 
Weapons
The general rule for assigning weapons to a character is to examine the sculpt of the associated model; if it has a weapon, then it qualifies to have a weapon assigned to it. 
ÔÇß	Each weapon type may have a variety of choices available; a player could decide that a model's over-large knife is a Dagger, a Saber, or maybe a Sword. 
ÔÇß	Characters pay full BP value for the highest cost Range Combat (Bow, Firearm, Range, Support, Thrown, Throwable) and Close Combat (Daggers, Clubs, Swords, etc.) weapons; other weapons are half-cost. Round up to nearest BP.
ÔÉº	Some models may have two or more weapons sculpted onto them; a player can choose to pay for just those intended for use. A weapon not paid-for can't be used. 
ÔÉº	Models with no weapons in their sculpt may be created as having either Improvised or Unarmed, with Improvised as the default which costs zero BP.
ÔÉº	Weapons with the Conceal or Discrete traits allow one purchase regardless of if the model has the proper sculpt.

Reading the Weapons List: 
Any entries which have only a dash (-) are considered to either not be relevant (such as for OR) or be empty (such as for Traits). Each entry in the various Weapon lists is described with these properties:
ÔÅ∂	Name ‚Äî The name of the weapon. Some weapons have name modifiers such as ‚ÄúSmall‚Äù, ‚ÄúLarge‚Äù, or ‚ÄúMedium‚Äù. Weapons which have ‚ÄúMedium‚Äù as part of their name are the default and can be written without that modifier. Therefore, ‚ÄúSpear‚Äù and ‚ÄúSpear, Medium‚Äù are identical.

Performing Tests
Dice & Modifiers
MEST Tactics uses three kinds of dice for all Tests; Base, Modifier, and Wild dice. These are regular six-sided dice with faces numbered 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, and 6. Some dice have ‚Äúpips‚Äù instead of numbers and these rules reference that.
ÔÇß	Modifier dice are red. Face values 1, 2, 3 is zero successes, and 4, 5, 6 is one success. Modifier dice are specified as +1 or Àó1 Modifier die, or as +1m or Àó1m.
ÔÇß	Base dice are white. Face values 1, 2, 3 is zero successes, and 4, 5, is one success, and 6 is two successes. Base dice are specified as +1 or Àó1 Base die, or as +1b or Àó1b. 
ÔÇß	Wild dice are yellow. Face values 1, 2, 3 is zero successes, and 4, 5, is one success, and 6 is three successes. Wild dice are specified as +1 or Àó1 Wild die, or as +1w or Àó1w. 
Types of Tests
Tests are either Opposed or Unopposed; two players are always involved in scoring dice in both. Opposed tests are used when two characters are involved. Unopposed tests are for all else. Tests are ‚Äúperformed‚Äù, and completed Tests are ‚Äúresolved‚Äù.
ÔÇß	The character performing an action that requires a test is the Active character. The target of that action is usually one or more models, or perhaps a terrain element or location upon the playing area.
ÔÇß	The player with the Active character performs the tests usually as the Attacker and the other player performs it on behalf of the target and is known as the Defender or Passive player.
ÔÇß	Some tests are given a familiar name such as ‚ÄúMorale Test‚Äù to mean an Unopposed POW Test. 
Opposed Tests
Each player rolls two Base dice and adds their character‚Äôs value for the matching attribute. 
If the test is identified with two Attributes; the player with the Active character adds the value for the first attribute and the other player adds the Passive character‚Äôs value for the second attribute. 
ÔÅ∂	For example; RCA vs. REF Test indicates that the Active character uses RCA while the target character uses REF.
ÔÅ∂	For example; Opposed INT Test indicates that both the Active character and the target use INT.
Unopposed Tests
If an Unopposed Test is identified, the Passive player merely rolls dice on behalf of the game system (‚ÄúSystem‚Äù) and adds 2 to the total. 
Have the Active player add any named Attribute values accordingly.
Bonus and Penalty Dice
Many traits will identify bonus and penalty dice to be used under specific Test conditions. Situational Test Modifiers associated with certain Actions will also identify dice.
ÔÇß	Tests may identify bonus or penalty dice. Whenever a character is penalized a die, a ‚Äúpenalty die‚Äù, what this means is that the Opposing player is awarded the die instead. 
For example; a +1m Combat Test provides +1 Modifier die to the Active character.
For example; a Àó1m Attacker Close Combat Test awards the Defender character because penalty dice will award the other character instead. 
ÔÇß	Bonus and penalty dice can be identified to affect more than one test. Notice how the wording on each of these changes how specific it is, and to which tests they may apply to:
ÔÉº	+1m Attacker Range Combat Hit Test
ÔÉº	+1m Attacker Combat Hit Test
ÔÉº	+1m Attacker Combat Test
ÔÉº	+1m Combat Test
ÔÉº	+1m Test
Flattening Dice
To allow more extremes values, the concept of flattening dice is used which reduces the total number of dice rolled.
ÔÇß	If both players receive the same kind of dice for tests, then they cancel each other out and are removed in equal numbers from each player.
ÔÇß	Flattening always occurs last; after all dice have been assigned, awarded, or adjusted by traits.
ÔÇß	The last two Base dice for each Player are never removed; they are not ‚Äúflattened‚Äù. Tests are always performed with at least two Base dice for each player.
 
Scoring Tests
When scoring tests (Unopposed or Opposed), players roll their two Base dice plus any number of additional dice according to whichever traits, weapons or situational modifiers occur. Add a relevant Attribute value as well.
ÔÇß	A die is scored as a single success when its face-value after rolling is 4 or higher. It is a miss otherwise.
ÔÇß	Base dice score 2 successes if the face-value is 6. 
ÔÇß	Wild dice always score 3 successes if the face-value is 6.
ÔÇß	A player's test score, ‚ÄúTest Score‚Äù, is their character's Attribute value plus all successes scored by the dice. 
ÔÇß	A Test is passed when the Active character has an equal to or greater Test Score than the other player. Otherwise, the test has failed.
Ties
As a general rule; Active characters always win ties.
Cascades, Misses, and Carry-overs
ÔÇß	Cascades are the difference in Test Scores in favor of the Active character. 
ÔÇß	Misses are what would have been needed to pass. 
ÔÇß	Active characters always win ties, receiving a cascade.
ÔÇß	Carry-overs are dice that might become available as bonuses for the Active character in the next test for the current action. 
ÔÉº	Modifier dice ‚Äúcarry-over‚Äù as bonus Modifier dice if the face-value of the die is 6. 
ÔÉº	Base dice ‚Äúcarry-over‚Äù as bonus Base dice if the face-value of the die is 6. 
ÔÉº	Wild dice scoring at least one success always ‚Äúcarry-over‚Äù as bonus Wild dice automatically. This is on a 4, 5, or 6.
ÔÉº	Carry-overs are from Hit Tests into Damage Tests when performing Combat, and from Link Tests into Weave Tests when performing Magic. No other Tests qualify.
ÔÉº	The System player never receives carry-overs.

Measuring
One Measured Unit [ MU ] is equal to the base-diameter of a SIZ 3 model; the standard human-sized character.
This is presumed to be a 30MM [ or 1.25-inch ] circular base for 1:64 to 1:48 scale [ 25MM to 33MM ] models. MU thus changes according to scale. Models on longer bases have a base-diameter equal to that longer length.
An MU is also written as straight double-quotes and the notations are interchangeable. Therefore 1" and  2" being 1 MU and 2 MU respectively. MU (pronounced ‚Äúem you‚Äù) is used interchangeably with ‚Äúunits‚Äù, ‚Äúsegments‚Äù, ‚Äúspaces‚Äù, ‚Äúsections‚Äù, but also ‚Äúinches‚Äù.
Overview

ÔÉº	Mark the stick with 1 MU segments. Also mark it with a 0.5-MU segment.
ÔÉº	Players may use one ruler-length to measure angle and distance at any time during play from their own models to elsewhere.
ÔÇß	Measuring from a model is from its base up to its height. This forms a cylindrical volume for which all Line-of-Sight [ LOS ] is to be traced towards or from. Its ‚Äúvisible area‚Äù.
ÔÉº	Models sculpted or mounted in unusual positions, or raised bases, must be treated as though of normal height which for a SIZ 3 model is equal to its base-diameter. 
ÔÉº	Long, flat, sprawling, overly tall, or spindly models can be regarded as tall as its tallest part. Have all players agree.
ÔÇß	Imprecision ‚Äî All measuring fractions round down to nearest 0.5". Positional adjustments up to 0.5" is allowed, favoring the Active model, if players agree. Games resolve faster this way.

Environment & Terrain
These are the standard rules for setting up a Battlefield.
A players‚Äô first battlefield may just be a table or desk with books and cups strewn about. Or it could have lovingly crafted terrain for hills, trees, buildings, rivers. Regardless there are certain guidelines that should be followed to help keep the pace of a game quick and interesting.
The Battlefield
For these QSR rules, the battlefield will always be Small and measure 24 x 24 MU.
Terrain Elements
Battlefields comprise of a good surface area known as a ‚ÄúField‚Äù. Place enough terrain to make the battlefield interesting with enough room to maneuver.
ÔÇß	Provide numerous side-channels multiple paths for movement through the battlefield.
ÔÇß	Limit open areas to no more 8" in length maximum otherwise range combat will dominate the game-play.
ÔÇß	If the genre encourages lots of range combat or has characters with high movement rates; try using a larger battlefield or start deployment from the corners of the play-area instead.
ÔÇß	If there are too many choke-points, game sessions will slow down if most of the characters use range combat.
ÔÇß	Keep the width between terrain elements least 2" so that models may fit between, and for tall terrain at least 4" so that players hands may fit within them.
Categories of Terrain
Terrain is categorized in one or more of; Walls, Obstacles, Areas, or Fields.
ÔÇß	Fields ‚Äî Relatively smooth flat terrain upon the battlefield.
ÔÇß	Walls ‚Äî Terrain with large surfaces that rise vertically. 
ÔÇß	Obstacles ‚Äî Everything not Fields but small enough to navigate around.
ÔÇß	Area ‚Äî Areas of the battlefield that have an agreed general effect, usually to act as Cover terrain, to be Rough terrain, or even both.

Terrain can affect how well models may traverse it, and whether models can hide or become obscured from view.

Cover is status for when a targeted model or location is partially obscured by terrain between it and an Active model. The term Cover includes concealment effects.

ÔÇß	Blocking terrain prevents LOS and is likely to stop all Damage by a given attack. 
ÔÇß	Trace LOS (a Line-of-Sight, a line) between the Active model‚Äôs visible area to the target‚Äôs visible area noting any terrain that line may cross.
ÔÉº	The visible area of a model is its base-width by its base-height. This is a cylinder if the model has a circular base.
ÔÉº	If the target is not a model but is a building or is terrain; its visible area is all of its front, facing the Active model.
ÔÉº	If the LOS is unobstructed at all positions to the target from all positions of the Active model; then there is no Cover; it is a Clear LOS. Otherwise, there may be Cover.
Cover & Cover Benefits
ÔÇß	A target is behind Cover if, from the perspective of an Opposing model, it is partially obscured by about half of its visible area by one or more terrain elements. It must be closer and of same SIZ or lower than any Opposing model if this Cover affects only base-height.
ÔÇß	One or more of these may apply, at most once each:
ÔÉº	Direct Cover is any terrain element that is in base-contact with the target, allowing some room for imprecision.
ÔÉº	Hard Cover is Direct Cover which could stop all of an attack‚Äôs damage if it was itself targeted directly. Anything that is not Hard Cover is by default Soft Cover.
ÔÉº	Intervening Cover is any terrain element that partially obscures the target. This includes any terrain that serves as Direct Cover for the Attacker. LOF to a target which crosses the base of a Distracted model always cause Intervening Cover.
ÔÇß	Intervening Cover ‚Äî Penalize the Active character -1 Modifier die for the Attacker Hit or Detect Test if target has Intervening Cover.
ÔÇß	Direct Cover ‚Äî Penalize the Active character -1 Base die for the Attacker Hit or Detect Test if target has Direct Cover.
ÔÇß	Hard Cover ‚Äî Assign the target character +1 Wild die for the Defender Damage Test if it has Hard Cover.
ÔÇß	Leaning (ÔÅ≤)‚Äî A character may establish LOS near its terrain, such as around a corner, or outside a window, etc. See Agility in the Performing Movement section.
Terrain Effects on Movement
ÔÇß	Terrain is graded as Clear ÔÇ¶ Rough ÔÇ¶ Difficult ÔÇ¶ Impassable and degrades in that order. 
ÔÉº	Fields, by default, are Clear.
ÔÉº	Walls, by default, are Impassable.
ÔÉº	Models entering or starting within Difficult terrain must stop, or acquire a Delay token to continue movement.
ÔÉº	Movement in Difficult or Rough terrain costs 2" for each 1" crossed. 
ÔÇß	Most placed Terrain elements are at least Rough.
ÔÉº	Hills, ramps, and staircases usually have a rise over run of 1 MU every 2 MU; this is the default and is Rough going up but Clear going down.
ÔÉº	Ladders are Rough terrain going up or down. 
ÔÉº	Characters can climb up ropes as Difficult terrain but down as Rough. 
ÔÉº	Both require two Hands [2H] going up, and one Hand [1H] going down.
ÔÇß	Doorways, doors, and windows:
ÔÉº	Open doors and any doorways are Clear. If these are of half base-height or half base-diameter or smaller, then regard them as Rough. 
ÔÉº	Open windows are Difficult, but Impassable if smaller than half base-diameter or base-height.
ÔÉº	If a model can't lay flat upon a terrain element without slipping or falling, or if its base or the model itself doesn't fit into within a portion of a terrain element; then it shouldn't be placed there. Players should identify exceptions where necessary.
Area Terrain (ÔÅ≤)
Area Terrain are patches of terrain placed upon the battlefield representing either Rough, Difficult, or even Impassable terrain. Add keywords for Obscuring or Uneven. Additional terrain elements, such as Obstacles or Walls, can always be placed atop Area terrain.
ÔÇß	Area terrain should be large enough to be significant. 
ÔÇß	Area terrain must specify its Movement effect; Clear, Rough, or Difficult. Use Clear by default.
ÔÇß	If necessary, demarcate the area of the terrain using buildings, trees, furniture, or something else.
Obscuring X ‚Äî Combine with any other Area terrain. If LOS crosses into at least 1 MU of any Obscuring terrain, the target receives +1 Modifier die Defender Range Combat Hit Test. 
ÔÉº	For every 1 MU that a model is within this terrain, the Visibility OR to it is reduced by X MU. 
ÔÉº	Models at higher elevation ignore the first 1" to a target into Area Terrain per 1" higher for every 1" distant.
Uneven X ‚Äî Combine with any other Area terrain.
ÔÉº	Penalizes -1 Modifier die Close Combat Hit Tests.
ÔÉº	The Surefooted trait has no benefit for Uneven terrain.
Playing the Game
Preparation
There are several Standard Missions, and the default one described here is ‚ÄúMission 1 > Elimination‚Äù.
Agreement
ÔÇß	Game Size ‚Äî Games are either Small, Medium, or Large. This determines the number of Build Points [ BP ] each player is given to assemble their forces, the number of models allowed, and the size of the battlefield. 
Game Size	BP per Side	Models per Side	Battlefield
Small	500	4 to 8	24 √ó 24 MU
Medium	750	6 to 12	36 √ó 36 MU
Large	1000	8 to 16	48 √ó 48 MU
ÔÇß	Mission Roles ‚Äî Each player rolls a six-sided die, re-rolling until there no ties. The player with highest scoring die decides which Side is the Mission Attacker.
Assemblies
ÔÇß	Sides ‚Äî Players form mutually opposing ‚ÄúSides‚Äù. Each Side has at least one player and all of their models. 
ÔÇß	Assemblies ‚Äî Spend BP to build Assemblies of Characters purchased from the various archetypes. 
ÔÇß	Equip ‚Äî Assign weapons, armor, and equipment. Meet the minimum and maximum model limits, and the BP limit. Keep the final cost within 25 BP of each other.
Battlefield
ÔÇß	Terrain ‚Äî Provide for enough terrain to make the game-play interesting. Terrain should be varied. 
ÔÉº	There should be Soft Cover and Hard Cover, Blocking, Uneven, and Obscuring terrain, Rough, and many Walls or Obstacles. Buildings are a nice touch as well. If desired, place and identify other Area Terrain elements.
ÔÉº	The battlefield should be created according to the rules identified in the Environment & Terrain section.
ÔÇß	Environ ‚Äî Mission Attacker picks Lighting as being ‚ÄúDay, Clear‚Äù or ‚ÄúTwilight, Overcast‚Äù, or ‚ÄúNight, Clear‚Äù. This gives Visibility OR of 16, 8, or 4 MU, respectively.
ÔÇß	Starting Zones ‚Äî The Mission Defender picks one of the Battlefield edges, and the Mission Attacker chooses the opposite edge.
Deployment
ÔÇß	The Mission Attacker decides who deploys first. 
ÔÉº	The Mission Defender decides which battlefield edge they‚Äôll deploy their own models. 
ÔÉº	The Mission Attacker will normally be assigned the opposite battlefield edge. 
ÔÇß	Deploy models within 2", 4", or 8" of their Battlefield edges for a Small, Medium, or Large game.
ÔÇß	Each model not in LOS of any Opposing models, or behind Cover, may start as Hidden. 
Start of Turn
Games are played in Turns alternating activations of characters with each player taking a Round. One player in each Side at the start of the Turn will perform the Initiative Test, and starts the Initiative Order. 
Readying
ÔÇß	Be sure to have a large six-sided die or something to indicate the current Turn number. A ‚ÄúTurn Indicator‚Äù.
ÔÉº	This begins at ‚Äò1‚Äô.
ÔÉº	Place it at the Mission Attacker‚Äôs battlefield edge.
ÔÇß	At the start of each Turn, remove all Done tokens from all models; this makes all In-Play characters become Ready.
Determine Initiative
ÔÇß	Have players representing each Side identify an In-Play Ordered character as their temporary Designated Leader.
ÔÉº	Perform an Initiative Test as an Opposed INT Test. 
ÔÉº	If no characters qualify, assume that Side‚Äôs Test Score is zero.
ÔÇß	Apply the ‚ÄúHindrance‚Äù Situational Test Modifiers.
ÔÇß	The Player with the highest Test Score is the winner and is awarded Initiative Points [ IP ] equal to the difference to the lowest Test Score. 
ÔÉº	If there is a tie, break it with highest total pips shown on the dice. Re-roll these tie-breakers using a six-sided die until there are no ties.
ÔÉº	All Players, except the winner, acquire an IP for each of their Base dice which have a carry-over.
ÔÉº	The winner decides to go first or picks a player to go first.
ÔÉº	The Mission Attacker may always choose to win the tie and the Initiative but as a result receive zero IPs.
ÔÇß	Optimized ‚Äî At the start of the first Turn, the Side with the least amount of BP spent building their Assembly is awarded +1 Base die for their Initiative Test.
The Initiative Card (ÔÅ≤)
There is a single Initiative card, and it is assigned to the Mission Attacker at the start of any Missions. The card can be a Joker or Ace from a standard Poker deck.
ÔÇß	The Initiative card is automatically transferred to the winner of the Initiative Test. 
ÔÇß	However, the player with the card may use it as follows:
ÔÉº	As an option, automatically win any tied Initiative Test but receive zero cascades in return. This rule supersedes the rule allowing the Mission Attacker to have the choice.
ÔÉº	Voluntarily transfer ownership to one of the involved Opposing players to re-roll any Test except for Initiative.
ÔÉº	If the Initiative card is transferred back to a Side which already had the card for current Initiative, do not allow voluntary transfer again for the remainder of this Initiative. Assign a Delay token to a Friendly model.
Situational Awareness (ÔÅ≤)
Each Turn one or more members of a player‚Äôs Assembly may or may not be in the right position on the battlefield for a Designated Leader to make decisions. This is known as ‚ÄúSituational Awareness‚Äù and it simulates how a leader uses that information to issue verbal commands and issue hand-signals to guide fellow Assembly members in resolving a Mission.
ÔÇß	This affects the Designated Leader character when performing the Initiative Test.
ÔÉº	Always check Situational Awareness after a Side has been reduced to less than half its original model count. 
ÔÉº	Do not check Situational Awareness on Turn 1.
ÔÇß	Situational Awareness has a maximum Awareness range for any given Designated Leader up to Visibility √ó 3. Use Visibility √ó 1 if the Designated Leader is Distracted.
ÔÇß	If the Designated Leader has less than half of its forces within LOS and Awareness range, counting itself, do not add its INT to the successes. 
ÔÉº	Characters with INT less than zero always add their INT.
ÔÉº	Friendly characters that are Hidden behind Cover from the Designated Leader are never counted.
Spending Initiative Points
IP received as a result of the Initiative Test can be spent at any time during a player‚Äôs own Round on Initiative Abilities as follows; 
ÔÅ∂	Maintain Initiative ‚Äî Pay 1 IP. Do not pass Initiative to another player; instead activate another Ready model.
ÔÅ∂	Force Initiative ‚Äî Pay 1 IP. Pass the Initiative to any other Opposing player. This is best used in multiplayer games to alter the order of Rounds until the end of the Turn.
ÔÅ∂	Refresh ‚Äî Pay 1 IP. When an Active model receives its Done token at the end of its Initiative, remove one of its Delay tokens. 
 
During the Turn
Players take Rounds in ‚ÄúInitiative Order‚Äù during a Turn which gives them opportunities to do something with one or more of their models.
Start Initiative
ÔÇß	The winner of the Initiative Test can decide to either go first, or forces another player on another Side to go first. That player receives the Initiative.
ÔÇß	The Player with the Initiative executes a Round by activating a Ready character or model. 
ÔÇß	That becomes the Active model, and it has the Initiative. The Player becomes the Active player, and all other players are Passive players.
Activation
ÔÇß	Players normally will be alternating activations for each of their models. The Passive Players will be able to perform React actions against Active characters.
ÔÇß	When a character is activated, it receives 2 Action Points [ AP ] to spend on one or more Actions. 
ÔÉº	This becomes an Activation and the current Action. 
ÔÉº	If it has Delay tokens, those must first be removed by spending 1 AP each. 
ÔÉº	See the Actions section for more information on how to deal with Actions.
ÔÇß	Some Opposing characters during this time may want to interrupt with their own actions using Reacts. See the React Actions section for more information on this.
End Round
ÔÇß	After a character finishes its Initiative, mark it with a Done token; it is no longer Ready. 
ÔÇß	The Initiative now passes; the next Side and one of its players then receives the Initiative and takes a Round.
ÔÇß	In 3+ Side games, the Initiative player picks which Side goes next; keep this order until the end of the Turn. This becomes the Initiative Order. Distribute and use numbered cards if necessary.
ÔÇß	Players on the same Side rotate taking activations each Round in which their Side receives the Initiative. 
ÔÇß	Repeat these Rounds until all characters are marked with a Done token. If this is the case, the Turn ends.
 
End of Turn
After all models have been marked as Done status, the Turn has ended.
ÔÇß	At the end of the Turn, all Initiative Points not spent are lost. 
ÔÇß	If at the end of a Turn it is determined that more than half of a player's forces have been KO‚Äôd or Eliminated, ‚ÄúBreakpoint Morale‚Äù has been reached and a Bottle Test is required by that player. See section for Morale, Fear, and Rally Tests. 
End of Game
After the End of Turn is resolved, all players check to see if the game has ended. 
End of Conflict
If there are no remaining Opposing models, the game ends. This usually happens in a two-player game or a game with two Sides and all Opposing models have been Eliminated or KO‚Äôd.
End-game Trigger
An End-game Trigger represents a culminating event which forces the conclusion of a Mission. This will vary by which Mission played but it is usually that the Mission Attacker has received overwhelming reinforcements or that the Mission Defender has finished a delaying tactic.
Use the Game Size per the total BP and adjust one row toward the total Models used to determine after which Turn the End-game Triggers are to be placed.
Game Size	Models per Side	BP per Side	End-game Begins
Small	4 to 8	500	4
Medium	6 to 12	750	6
Large	8 to 16	1000	8
ÔÇß	At the end of each Turn, if there are End-game Trigger dice (‚ÄúEND die‚Äù); have the Mission Defender roll them. 
ÔÉº	END dice are regular six-sided dice, numbered 1 to 6.
ÔÉº	If any END die scores 1, 2, or 3 (a ‚Äúmiss‚Äù), then the game ends immediately.
ÔÇß	If the game has ended, check Victory Conditions.
ÔÇß	If the game has not ended:
ÔÉº	Add an END die if it is Turn 4, 6, or 8 (for Small, Medium, or Large games) or later.
ÔÉº	Increase the Turn number by 1 and repeat the game-play sequence at Start of Turn.
 
Victory Conditions
Each Mission will provide information on when a game will end and the way to determine the winner with a set of Victory conditions. 
During the course of a game turn, and at the end of the game, all players check to see if they‚Äôve acquired Resource Points [ RP ] and Victory Points [ VP ]. The highest VP total determine the winner of each game session; the player or Side with the highest VP total wins.
Track RP and VP using counters or six-sided dice.
Overview
Unless specified otherwise, all game sessions use these default victory conditions for acquiring VPs named as Aggression, Bottled, Elimination, and Outnumbered.
ÔÅ∂	Aggression ‚Äî +1 VP if at least half of the Assembly‚Äôs models, by starting count, cross an imaginary line across the middle of the battlefield. Award +1 RP to the first model to cross.
ÔÅ∂	Bottled ‚Äî +1 VP if Opposing Side failed Bottle Test [ ‚ÄúBottled Out‚Äù ] or has no Ordered characters. If there at least two Opposing Sides remaining in play, reward those sides each +3 Resource Points instead each time a Side ‚ÄúBottled Out‚Äù.
ÔÅ∂	Elimination ‚Äî +1 VP if have most Opposing KO‚Äôd and Eliminated characters by BP total at game end. Doesn‚Äôt include models Bottled.
ÔÅ∂	Outnumbered ‚Äî +1 VP to the Side that is outnumbered 3:2 models or greater at start of game, but +2 if outnumbered 2:1 models or greater. If there are multiple Sides remaining at the end of the game, do not award this keyword.
Resource Points
Resource Points [ RPs ] are used as a secondary way to gain an additional VP and to also break ties in the highest VP totals. This can be a feature of some Victory Conditions or Missions, such as the Aggression keyword above.
ÔÇß	+1 VP to the Side awarded the most Resource Points. Having double the RP of the next Opposing Side, but by at least 10 RPs more, is +2 VP instead.
ÔÇß	If there is still a tie for VPs then the Side with the most total RPs win.
Observation
In the case where one Assembly outnumbers the other by 2:1 or greater, the smaller side already begins with +2 VP. 
The Side with the larger Assembly would need to both cause the smaller side to ‚ÄúBottle Out‚Äù and cause it to have a higher value of models Eliminated or KO‚Äôd in terms of BP just to merely have a tied game for most Missions.
 
Actions
Individual Actions
Actions are classified as Simple, Special, Complex and their frequency of use follows that order. When learning these rules, learn actions by class in that same order.
Initiative & Activation
ÔÇß	When a player receives the Initiative, they begin their Round as the Active player and receive 2 Action Points 
[ AP ] to spend on an In-Play Ready character of their choice. That character, and its model, becomes the Active character and model; it becomes Activated and receives the Initiative. 
ÔÇß	A character receives the Initiative just once per Turn, but may become Activated and be the Active character many times in order to perform Actions. It may even become Active during another character‚Äôs Initiative.
ÔÉº	When a character becomes Active but has Delay tokens, those must first be removed by spending 1 AP each. 
ÔÉº	The controlling player can then announce and then spend the character‚Äôs remaining APs by picking from the available Simple, Special, or Complex actions shown in the following lists. Most Actions are 1 AP each.
ÔÉº	Actions can be aborted before any Tests are performed.
ÔÇß	After a character has finished its activations for its Initiative, mark it with a Done token.
ÔÇß	Note that some traits and statuses may cause characters to have compulsory actions. See the section on Morale, Fear, and Rally Tests.
Pushing
Once per Initiative, at the option of the player; Active characters having no Delay tokens may use ‚ÄúPushing‚Äù to push themselves to their limit and acquire 1 AP. They will also immediately acquire a Delay token.
Reacts (ÔÅ≤)
Individual Actions may be interrupted by Opposing models which use Reacts; allow each player time to announce any Reacts. See the section on Reacts for more information.
Using Agility (ÔÅ≤)
Agility is a feature of Movement actions which can be used during actions to navigate difficult or unusual terrain. A character normally has Agility equal to half of MOV in MU. 
Agility can also be used for when determining LOS. 
See the section on Performing Movement Actions on how to use Agility.
 
Simple Actions
ÔÇß	Move ‚Äî If Free pay 1 AP.

ÔÉº	If Free, move model up to its MOV + 2" acknowledging terrain and Agility limitations; stop if become Engaged with Attentive Opposing model. 
ÔÉº	Must move in straight segments but may perform up to MOV direction changes during the course of movement. Allow an additional facing change before the use of any trait with the Movement keyword.
ÔÉº	Allow models to use Swap Positions when qualified.
ÔÉº	See the section on Performing Movement for more information.
ÔÇß	Close Combat Attack ‚Äî Pay 1 AP. Attack a model within Melee Range and LOS as the target. Unless using a Natural weapon, acquire a Delay token if this is not the character‚Äôs first attack this Initiative. 
ÔÉº	Must use a weapon identified as ‚ÄúMelee‚Äù or ‚ÄúNatural‚Äù. Other Items can be used as an Improvised Melee weapon. 
ÔÉº	Allow models to use Overreach when qualified.
ÔÉº	Apply Situational Test Modifiers as necessary.
ÔÉº	There is a Hit Test and upon passing, also a Damage Test.
See the section on Performing Close Combat for more information.
ÔÇß	Range Combat Attack ‚Äî Pay 1 AP. If Free, attack a location or a model as the target, using a weapon that has an OR value. ÔÉº	Must use a weapon identified with an OR value such as ‚ÄúThrown‚Äù, ‚ÄúBow‚Äù, ‚ÄúFirearm‚Äù, ‚ÄúSupport‚Äù, or ‚ÄúRange‚Äù. Other Items can be used as Improvised Thrown weapon.
ÔÉº	Thrown weapons, and weapons with the Throwable trait have an OR equal to the character's STR. 
ÔÉº	Apply Situational Test Modifiers as necessary.
ÔÉº	There is a Hit Test and upon passing, also a Damage Test. 
See the section on Performing Direct Range Combat for more information.
ÔÇß	Disengage ‚Äî Pay 1 AP. If Engaged, perform this as a +1 Base die Close Combat Hit Test, but using REF vs. CCA instead, with an Ordered Opposing target to which this model is Engaged, as selected by the Opposing player. 
ÔÉº	Apply Situational Test Modifiers as necessary.
ÔÉº	Upon pass, reposition the Active model MOV √ó 1".
ÔÉº	Presume the Active character is the Defender.
See the section on Performing Disengage for more information.

ÔÉº	Apply Situational Test Modifiers as necessary.

ÔÉº	Apply Situational Test Modifiers as necessary.

ÔÉº	When in Wait status, and involuntarily acquire a Delay token, must remove both instead.
ÔÉº	‚ÄúFocus‚Äù ‚Äî Remove Wait status while Attentive to receive +1 Wild die for any Test instead of performing a React.
ÔÉº	‚ÄúWaiting‚Äù ‚Äî All characters in Wait status receive +1 REF when qualifying for a React.
 
Resolving Actions
There are several key Actions each player will continually perform with their characters during a Turn. These are Move and Attack and are covered here in detail:
Performing Movement
The Move action involves the MOV attribute to relocate a model across the battlefield. In regards to a model changing its location, this is a movement or a move.
Overview
ÔÇß	Allowance ‚Äî A model that is Free may move up to its MOV + 2" acknowledging terrain and Agility limitations. This is its normal ‚ÄúMovement Allowance‚Äù per Action point.
ÔÇß	Direction ‚Äî A model moves in a straight line, but may change direction up to MOV times during movement.
ÔÇß	Measuring ‚Äî To move a model, measure from its base at a leading edge in the direction of movement to the same edge. 
ÔÉº	Using a measuring stick with 1" segments to determine where any leading edge of the model could end. 
ÔÉº	All models move in a straight line for each AP spent on Movement. 
ÔÉº	Each MOV allows a single direction change during movement of any amount during this time. Allow an additional facing change before the use of any trait with the Movement keyword.
ÔÉº	If there are obstacles in the way such as pits or hedges, the player may use the rules on Agility to jump across. 
See the section on Agility for more information.
ÔÇß	Interrupts ‚Äî Whenever a model is interrupted via a React, temporarily place a pawn or some sort of indicator at that position for reference.

General Concerns
Random Selection
Whenever a player needs to perform a random selection against a table, use one or more six-sided dice.
ÔÅ∂	Y/N ‚Äî A ‚ÄúYes‚Äù or ‚ÄúNo‚Äù is checked using a single six-sided die or a MEST die. A pass (rolling a 4, 5, or 6) is a ‚ÄúYes‚Äù. Else fail.
ÔÅ∂	D6 ‚Äî Sometimes written as ‚Äú#‚Äù. A single six-sided die can be used against a table with just 6 entries. 
ÔÅ∂	D66 ‚Äî Also known as R+W or D66. A red six-sided die is rolled and used as the ‚Äútens‚Äù column and a white six-sided die is rolled and as the ‚Äúones‚Äù column. These can be rolled together.
ÔÅ∂	D666 ‚Äî Also known as RWY or D666. As before, the red six-sided die is read first and becomes the ‚Äúhundreds‚Äù column, white is ‚Äútens‚Äù, and yellow is ‚Äúones‚Äù.
Falling (ÔÅ≤)
A model is considered falling if it goes downwards from a ledge for a distance further than its Agility. 
ÔÇß	If within the last 0.5 MU of Agility or more, acquire a Wound token. If more than Agility, also acquire a Delay token.  
ÔÇß	Perform a Falling Test as an Unopposed FOR Test DR SIZ and increase that DR by 1 per 4 MU beyond Agility fallen. Round to the nearest whole number.
ÔÇß	If it fails, assign misses Delay tokens as Stun damage.
ÔÇß	Collision ‚Äî When falling into other models, the falling model may ignore one miss. The targets must then perform a Falling Test using the DR that was used for the falling model.
 
For Positioning
Charge (ÔÅ≤)
Characters that move before causing an Opposing character to become within Melee Range may qualify for the ‚ÄúCharge Bonus‚Äù as follows; 
ÔÇß	Charge ‚Äî The Active character receives +1 Modifier die for the Attacker Close Combat Hit Test if it performed a Move action into base-contact with its target, over Clear terrain from a Free position in a relatively straight line.
ÔÇß	Qualification ‚Äî All of these conditions must be met:
ÔÉº	The Move action must have cost at least 1 AP.
ÔÉº	The Active character must start Free from at least its own base-diameter away before the distance of possibly becoming Engaged by the target character or having the target become within Melee Range. 
ÔÉº	Movement must be directly into the target and make base-contact. Disallow direction changes during this Action.
ÔÉº	The target must not be Hidden and be within LOS at the start of the Action, at no more than Visibility √ó 3 ORM.
ÔÉº	Traits or Agility can make some terrain effectively Clear.
Swap Positions (ÔÅ≤)
This rule allows models to switch positions (‚ÄúSwap‚Äù) in congested Scrum configurations, which is especially helpful within buildings or dense terrain locations. 
ÔÇß	During movement, a model may swap positions with any Disordered Distracted models or Attentive Friendly Free models which are in base-contact. 
ÔÉº	Afterwards apply a Delay token to one of the non-Opposing models if any model was Disordered.
ÔÉº	The first Swap during an Initiative costs zero AP. Otherwise 1 AP each additional occurrence.
ÔÇß	Do not allow Swap if the target model is Engaged to an Attentive Ordered Opposing model.
Confinement (ÔÅ≤)
A model in base-contact with or near certain terrain for any two directions; horizontally, vertically above, or behind, is Confined.
ÔÉº	A model with the Reach trait, or with an Item ‚ÄúIn Hand‚Äù with the Reach trait must have at least half the Reach ability clear of Terrain for those directions.
ÔÉº	A model could be both Cornered and also Confined.
ÔÉº	Models upon Difficult terrain, or in base-contact with Impassable terrain count towards being Confined.
ÔÉº	Models near a precipice, with its base-diameter at or over the precipice are always regarded as Confined.
ÔÇß	Confined ‚Äî Penalize -1 Modifier die Close Combat Test or Defender Range Combat Hit Test if Confined by Terrain for any two; vertically, horizontally, or behind.
 
For Attacks

ÔÉº	if it is within 1" of LOF to the target

ÔÇß	Obscured ‚Äî Penalize Attacker Hit or Detect Tests for 1, 2, 5, or 10 other models within LOF to the target, and for non-Opposing models beyond but within 1 MU of LOF. Each is -1 Modifier die.

ÔÇß	Range Combat ‚Äî An Attacker performing Range Combat requires a weapon that has either the Throwable trait, have an Optimal Range [ OR ] entry, or be a Bow, Thrown, Range, Firearm, Energy, or Beam weapon.
ÔÇß	A weapon becomes an Improvised weapon when the requirements are not met. These become Large Improvised weapons if the original weapon had the [2H] trait.

The traits such as Bite, Claws, Gore, Lash, Quills, Slam, Stinger, Tentacles, or Trample are Natural weapons. Being Unarmed is also considered having a Natural weapon.
ÔÇß	Natural Weapons ‚Äî Are not penalized attacking more than once in a Turn.
ÔÉº	All Natural weapons have the [Stub] trait. This is penalty of -1 Modifier die Attacker Close Combat Hit Test. 
ÔÉº	May not use the Overreach option for attacking.
ÔÉº	Many Natural Weapons require [2H]. This would cause a Swordfighter with Claws and a Sword to use [3H], which is usually a penalty when interrupted for a React.
ÔÇß	Multiple Natural Weapons ‚Äî An archetype (Animal, Beast, Sophont, etc.) may be identified as having multiple Natural weapons to receive the Multiple Weapons benefit as described in that heading.
ÔÉº	Natural weapons benefit from this rule when explicitly written such as 2 √ó Claws or 3 √ó Bite. Each is a different ‚Äúin hand‚Äù weapon for purposes of using this rule.
ÔÉº	Characters with multiple Natural weapons, such as having both Bite and Claws, do qualify for the Multiple Weapons benefit. Each type counts as an ‚Äúin hand‚Äù weapon.

 
Multiple Weapons (ÔÅ≤)
Characters benefit from the Multiple Weapons rule if their model is shown as sculpted showing this and the weapons are also purchased using BP. 
ÔÇß	Qualification ‚Äî Weapons in each hand must be all capable of Ranged attacks (being Bow, Thrown, etc.), or all capable of Melee attacks. 
ÔÉº	Weapons are classified as either Melee or Ranged. Melee weapons with the Throwable trait count as Ranged weapons, and Ranged with [Awkward] count as Melee.
ÔÉº	Natural weapons are their own sub-classification.
ÔÉº	Improvised weapons do not count for this rule.
ÔÇß	Assessment ‚Äî Characters should be clearly sculpted with the weapon ‚Äúin hand‚Äù unless the weapon has the Conceal or Discrete traits. 
ÔÉº	Count each additional hand which is armed with a Ranged or Melee weapon. Models might for example have ‚Äúin hand‚Äù Bite and Claws, or Trample and Gore.
ÔÉº	Count each additional pair of claws or pair of fists, or additional independent tail, or additional independent mouth used for biting.

ÔÇß	Benefit ‚Äî When targeting the same model:
ÔÉº	Each additional Ranged weapon ‚Äúin hand‚Äù provides +1 Modifier die Attacker Range Combat Tests. 
ÔÉº	Each additional Melee weapon ‚Äúin hand‚Äù provides +1 Modifier die Close Combat Tests.
ÔÇß	Penalty ‚Äî Using the same weapon in consecutive Actions during an Initiative penalizes -1 Modifier die Attacker Combat Tests if there are others of the same classification already ‚Äúin hand‚Äù.
ÔÇß	Interruptions ‚Äî A character that is using a specific weapon for an attack which is then interrupted, such as during Reacts or Passive Player Options, must use that weapon for defense as well.

These rules provide choices for deeper game play. 
Passive Player Options (ÔÅ∞)
Passive Player Options, named with an exclamation mark (!), are a limited form of React actions, and are classified similarly as ‚Äúinterrupts‚Äù. These options are given primarily to the target of a Close Combat or Range Combat attack by an Active model. 
ÔÇß	This does not include targets of any Disengage Test, and are not available against Reacts, against Group Actions, nor against Bonus Actions.

Optional Responses
When the Attacker has failed its Combat Hit Test, an Attentive Defender targeted by the attack is allowed one of these Optional Responses each Turn:
ÔÇß	Counter-strike! ‚Äî When an Attacker fails its Hit Test an Attentive Ordered Defender with the Attacker in Melee Range may immediately perform a Damage Test. 
ÔÉº	It acquires a Delay token, but if it was in Wait status remove that instead.

ÔÇß	Counter-fire! ‚Äî When an Attacker fails its Direct Range Combat Hit Test a Free Attentive Ordered Defender in Visibility may immediately perform a Damage Test.
ÔÉº	The Attacker must have been Revealed and within LOS since the start of its Action.
ÔÉº	The Defender must have an equal or higher REF.
ÔÉº	It acquires a Delay token, but if it was in Wait status remove that instead.
ÔÉº	Reverse roles, and have it perform a Range Combat Damage Test as the Active model. It receives any of its carry-overs from the Hit Test. Upon pass, allow it any Bonus Actions if qualified.
ÔÇß	Counter-action! ‚Äî Allow an Attentive Ordered Defender of any failed Close Combat Hit Test or Range Combat Hit Test to perform Bonus Actions. 
ÔÉº	The Defender must have at least one carry-over, and must have equal or higher REF if this is Range Combat.
ÔÉº	It acquires a Delay token, but if it was in Wait status remove that instead. 
ÔÉº	Count the successes on any carry-overs as cascades for spending on Bonus Actions. Have it perform any Bonus Actions as the Active model.

ÔÇß	Counter-charge! ‚Äî Allow a single Free Attentive Ordered Passive model to respond to a model that is concluding a Move after spending 2 AP, or is concluding a Combined Action after spending 2 AP.
ÔÉº	If REF is higher, allow Counter-charge! after the target spends 1 AP spent for movement. 
ÔÉº	The Passive model may only perform this Counter-charge if it can move so that either model becomes Engaged.
ÔÉº	It acquires a Delay token, but if it was in Wait status remove that instead. 
ÔÉº	Have it perform a 1 AP Move action, but regard it as having been repositioned.
Optional Tactics
An Attentive Opposing model is allowed one of these choices each Turn whenever targeted for an attack:
ÔÇß	Opportunity Attack! ‚Äî An Active model which was Engaged and leaves without performing a Disengage action or Disengage Bonus Action is subject to any Opportunity Attack by one of its Opposing models.

ÔÇß	Defend! ‚Äî If the target of a Close Combat Hit Test is Attentive, its player may announce it will ‚ÄúDefend!‚Äù.
ÔÉº	This choice awards +1 Base die Defender Hit Test.

ÔÉº	Otherwise, the Active player may reposition the target and optionally the Active model as though using either the Reversal or Circle Bonus Actions. 
ÔÉº	If afterwards no attack can occur, the AP spent is lost.
ÔÉº	At the end of the Action, after resolving Tests, unless the Active model performed its Bonus Action, the target model acquires a Delay token. If it is in Wait status remove that instead.
 
ÔÇß	Take Cover! ‚Äî For Direct Range Combat, Free Attentive Ordered targets of the attack may ‚ÄúTake Cover!‚Äù.

ÔÉº	At the end of the Action, those models which repositioned acquire a Delay token. If they were in Wait status remove that instead.

Game Play Sequence
See the Playing the Game section for a complete description.
Start of Turn
Players should be made aware of the Victory Conditions. Remind at the start of each Turn.
ÔÇß	Ready all In-Play models by removing all Done tokens.
ÔÇß	Check Initiative
ÔÉº	Check Situational Awareness
ÔÉº	Perform Initiative Test
ÔÉº	Assign Initiative Points [IP]
ÔÉº	Winner determines which player receives the Initiative.
Take Round
Initiative Points may be spent during an Initiative.
ÔÇß	Player with the Initiative activates a Ready character and assigns it 2 AP, but removes 1 AP per Delay token it has.
ÔÉº	Spend any number of its remaining AP on Actions.
ÔÉº	If the character is not Distracted, it may use Pushing for an additional AP.
ÔÉº	Mark the model with a Done token after it is done with all possible Actions the player wants to perform with it.
ÔÇß	Next Player takes a Round.
End of Round
Manage any game-effects at the end of each Round.;
End of Turn
When all models have been marked with a Done token, the Turn has ended. Several in-game tasks must be checked as follows;
ÔÇß	Discard all remaining IP.
ÔÇß	Check Bottle Tests
ÔÇß	Check for Victory Conditions
End of Game
Check if the game has ended by roll any End-game Trigger dice [ END ] and resolve that. If the game has not ended, add another END die, and then start a new Turn.

-1m	Cornered. Disengage and Hit Tests if Engaged to the Opposing model on one side of this model and in base-contact on the other side with a wall, precipice, or degraded terrain.
