# Materials Required for Play

Players need to provide various materials to begin a game.

## Dice

Acquire at least 6 dice in each of the three (red, white, yellow) dice colors (18 dice total).

*   Commonly available regular six-sided dice with pips (dots) numbering 1 to 6 will be adequate.
*   Use red for Modifier dice, white as Base dice, and yellow for Wild dice. These are also known as m, b, and w dice.

Have a few different colors of tiny utility six-side dice. These are used for various purposes such as for End-game dice, count-down timers, etc.

Optionally use a very large six-sided dice to track the current game-turn “Turn”. These dice are big so that all players can see them at a distance if necessary.

## Models

At least 4 models per player in a variety of sculpts.

We recommend 28MM or 1:56 scale figurines, but the game can support nearly any scale. These 1:56 scale models should be mounted upon 30MM (or use 1.25-inch) circular bases if human-sized (“Humanoid” SIZ 3).

If the models are already based upon something different such as ovals, squares or hexagons, this is acceptable; just presume the rules are written for those bases.

## Battlefield

Every battlefield will be dynamically configured by all players involved, using available terrain elements.

*   A play-area, or “battlefield” of at least 24" × 24" in dimensions. The ideal size is 48" × 48". Allow additional room for side-boarded cards, utilities, and play-aids.
*   Lots of terrain representing trees, buildings, hills, walls, and clutter. Allow 1 to 4 Items per 12"×12" play-area.

## Tools

*   A pair of measuring sticks 8 MU long divided into 1 MU segments. Each measured unit (1 MU) is about 1" long, which is the width of a standard circular base. At one end should be a marking at 0.5 MU.
*   Markers and tokens of various colors; beads are extremely useful for this purpose.
*   A couple of gaming pawns to serve as reminders.
*   Paper and pencils or pens for record keeping.
*   Although not necessary, it is very useful to have at least one laser-pointer to check “Line-of-Sight” [ LOS ] during game-play.
*   Another nice-to-have are tiny Japanese-style side-dish bowls for rolling dice in hand, or some dice-trays.

## Tokens

During game-play a character will succumb to one or more common status effects. Such information could be recorded on paper, but it is much more useful to identify models by placing colored glass beads as tokens, or status markers next to them.

If using character cards, place the tokens upon those cards to keep the play-area clutter-free.

Here are the colors and sizes that are recommended as the standard for play.

*   Done — Largest & blue
*   Wait — Big & white or clear
*   Hidden — Small & dark
*   Wound — Small & red
*   Delay — Small & white or clear
*   Fear — Small & yellow

## Markers

There should be some markers to indicate that a weapon (a bow or crossbow) is “Out-of-Ammo!” to represent that they must reload before using again. These are best represented using icons upon small chits, washers, or bingo chips which are about 20MM or 0.75” across.

There are also tactical resource types such as “Initiative Points”. These will be held near a player as spendable resources. These are best represented using icons upon larger elements which are about 30MM or 1.25” across, or larger.

## Proxies

Not every tabletop wargame enthusiast has all of the necessary game-pieces, models, and terrain elements to build a complete set for any tabletop wargame. For the most part, TTWGs can be played using proxies. Use cups and books for terrain, coins for models, or paper cut-outs for nearly everything. For models, good sources are the many available “dudes on a map” boardgames.

Having artful pieces representing each type of game element helps tremendously with immersion by putting less strain on the imagination, allowing players to focus on tactics and the rules which support that.
